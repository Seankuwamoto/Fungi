Hyphae

Almost all fungi have bodies composed of a network of tiny filaments, called hyphae (Fig. 2). They are typically a single cell thick, and are reinforced with chitin, the same material found in the exoskeletons of insects. Mycorrhizal hyphae are fungi that form mycorrhizae, which are mutually benifical relationships between a fungus and a plant's roots. Mycorrhizal hypae extract nutrients and water from deep within the soil and use their network to bring them to the plant, recieving sugars and other nutrients from the plant in return.

Mycelium!

The large, interconnected web of hyphae that a fungus has is known as a mycelium. Most mushrooms have mycelia that extend for many feet into the ground, where most of their mass is located. After all, the mycelium is the main body of the fungus, and mushrooms only pop out of the earth to spread their spores. Mycelia have maximal surface area to volume ratios, making their absorbing of nutrients very efficient. A single cubic centimeter of a mycelium can have as much as 1km of hyphae!

Types of Fungi - Overview

Fungi are a diverse kingdom of living creatures, with five main phyla:

  • Chytridiomycota
  • Zygomycota
  • Glomeromycota
  • Ascomycota
  • Basidiomycota
Each with their own diverse and unique characterics. Some are single celled, such as yeasts, and many are multicellular such as common grocery store mushrooms. One cool example of a basidiomycete is the Orange Pore Fungus (Fig. 5).

Figure 6. A microscopic zoospore

Types of Fungi - Chytrids

Fungi in the phylum Chytridiomycota, called chytrids, are commonly found in lakes and soil. There are approximately 1000 known species of chytrids, and they play various roles in the ecosystem, from decomposers, to parasites of fungi, plants, and animals. Some chytrids also play important mutually benifical roles, such as the chytrids found in the digestive tracts of sheep that help them digest plant matter. The thing that sets chytrids apart from other fungi is that they have flagellated spores (Fig. 6) known as zoospores. These zoospores aid parasitic chytrics that live in rivers, infecting amphibians such as frogs to steal nutrients from them.

Types of Fungi - Zygomycetes

Fungi in the phylum Zygomycota, called zygomycets, are often fast growing molds (Fig. 7), like the kind you would find on a piece of bread that was left on the counter for too long. One characteristic commonly found in zygomycetes is that their hyphae are coenocytic, meaning that they are not composed of multiple cells sitting side by side, but rather one large fused mass of cytoplasm that forms the whole organism with many nuclei. They reproduce with structures called zygosporangia, which are hard, ball-shaped containers which can resist harsh conditions for months. Once conditions are suitable, a zygosporangium will germinate and sprout a sporangium, which disperses many spores into the environment.

Figure 7. A close up of a pin mold (Mucorales) growing on a peach.

Types of Fungi - Glomeromycetes

Glomeromycetes, originally thought to be zygomycetes but later revealed to be a completely separate phylogenetic tree by recent molecular studies are a small phylum of fungi with only around 160 known species. Nearly every glomeromycete has arbuscular mycorrhizae, which are hyphae that form symbiotic relationships with plant roots by penetrating inside their cell walls, forming tiny arbuscules (Fig. 8). Suprisingly, about 90% of all plant species have mutualistic relationships with glomeromycetes! These micorrhizae help the plant by reaching deep into the earth to collect water with their hyphae and in return plants provide nutrients and minerals for them.

Types of Fungi - Ascomycetes

With a staggering 65,000 species known, ascomycetes (phylum Ascomycota) are a diverse set of fungi defined by their production of spores in a saclike ascus. Because of these unique spore sacs, these fungi are often referred to as sac fungi. Unlike zygomycota, which spread and cultivate their spores on the microscopic level, ascomycetes grow large, fruiting bodies, called ascocarps. These ascocarps are differnt from the fruiting bodies of most common mushrooms, which are basidiomycetes, in that its fruting body does not release its spores from a set of gills lined with basidia. Nonetheless, some common mushrooms are ascocarps, such as the morel (Fig. 9).

Figure 9. A common American morel mushroom, also known as Morchella americana.

Figure 10. Phallus indusiatus, a basidiomycete.

Types of Fungi - Basidiomycetes

Most well-known mushrooms fall into the category of basidiomycetes, classified in the phylum Basidiomycota. It includes funigi that form mycorrhizal relationships with vascular plants, as well as destructive plant parasites. The name comes from latin basidium, meaning little pedestal, referring to the pedastal like shape of their fruiting bodies.
Basidiomycetes are great decomposers, with some being especially well known for being able to break down lignin, a structural protein found in wood. Basidiomycetes form fruiting bodies called basidiocarps, examples of which are common white mushrooms found in supermarkets. Another example is Maiden veil fungus (Fig. 10) which has an odor similar to rotting meat.
